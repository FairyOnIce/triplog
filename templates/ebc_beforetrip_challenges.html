The Challenges Here are the lists of challenges that I faced:
  1. Getting more than 2 weeks of vacation days during the EBC trekking seasons for both my husband and me
  2. Physically prepared for high altitude environment
  3. Gather information and plan the affordable trip


+2 weeks vacations

The time required for EBC trekking vary across tours. The shortest trip to EBC seems to take about 7 - 8 days, and often has at most 1 acclimitization day. This route takes the same route in and out, and it is roughly like this:

  1. Lukla -> Namche Bazzar -> Tengboche -> Dingboche -> Lobuche -> Everest Base Camp -> Dingboche -> Lukla

(When I was planning the EBC trip, it was very difficult for me to remember the unfamiliar foreign geographical names. For someone like me, I embedded the google map around EBC with a geographical names at map and schedule page. This page essentially shows the route I decided to take. So please refer to it when you get overwhelmed with the geographical names.)

We did not want to take this route because the schedule seems too tight, and we wanted to make sure that we will have enough acclimitization days. We also thought that it would be nice that if the trekking route is not piston but a loop to enjoy more view.

Gyoko Lake

Gyoko Lake is a popular "adds-on" to the EBC trek. Going to Gyoko lake requires at least additional 2 - 3 days to the route but it can make the entire trek into a loop. Visiting EBC is once-in-life-time experience and who knows if we come back to this area on earth in the future? So we decided to add Gyoko lake in our route.

Clockwise loop or counter-clockwise loop

There are two ways to go to Gyoko lake and EBC: clockwise route and counter-clockwise route. The clockwise route would add at least 2 additional days to the route while the counter clockwise route seems to add at least 3 additional days. From online research we found that if we take a clockwise route, the route from Gokyo side is little though, and step walking up is required in comparisons to the counter-clockwise route from Everest base camp side. The trail from Everest base camp side is gradually ascent, so it is easier and more comfortable of doing Chola la pass from Everest base camp side rather than from Gokyo. From this information, we decided to visit Gyoko lake in counter-clockwise loop.


We also decided to have two additional acclimitization days and this made our EBC trip to extend 15 days. See Before trip for the exact route that we decided to take.

We overcame this first challenge by planning ahead, and saved vacation days in 2017.



Physical Preparation for High Altitude

I was concerened about the high altitude. The highest altitude that I visited was 4,207m in Mauna Kea, and I felt very weak when I was there. You get altitutde sickness especially when you are tired and did not have a good sleep. So in order to avoid this, we decdided to have extra buffer days to fix the jet lag in Kathumandu before our 15 trekking days.

Altitude sickness medicine

Diamox is the only clinically proven medication which speeds up acclimatization. However, it seems that we need prescription to purchase this medicine in US. So we decided to buy it in Kathmandu. Let's see if our decision will be right...



Gather information and plan the affordable trip


Deciding on tours

Gathering infomation online was one of the biggest challenge and that is also the reason why I was motivated to log my experience.

Tour vs individual trekking

We know that individual trekking is possible and potentially cheaper. But we decided to go with a tour because we are pretty new to this high altitude world, and wanted to have someone that we can rely on in the case of emergency. Nevertheless, this blog gave me a good ideas on individual trek.


Western tour vs local tour

The price of EBC tour vary between $700 - $5,000, and local tours are in the lower range (< $2,000) while the western tours are in higher range (> $2,000). My impression of the biggest difference in service was that western tour comes with western tour guide and "nice" tea house accomodation while the local tours come with local guide and "regular" accommodation. We decided to go with the local tour because we did not really care much about the nice accommodation in Nepal.


Choosing local tours

Bookmundi website was great help deciding on which local tours to select. You can chat with the tour organizers on live (for free) to negotiate the price and ask basic questions. I talked to 4 travel agencies (Green Valley Nepal Treks, Unique Path Trekking & Expedition, Nepal Eco Adventure Pvt Ltd and Himalayan Leisure, Inc.) and decided to go with Himarayan Leisure.



The service provided by most of the four agencies

All the 4 travel agencies offer packages that contains basic items, and they are:

  1. Airport pickup and drop services on a private vehicle before and after the trek
  2. Kathmandu - Lukla - Kathmandu domestic airline tickets
  3. All applicable domestic airport taxes
  4. Accommodation on cozy tea houses during the whole trek
  5. Trekkers Information Management System (TIMS) registration card
  6. Experienced English speaking, government licensed and ministry of tourism trained trekking guide with meals, accommodation, salary and insurance
  7. Down jacket, four seasonal sleeping bag, duffel bag and trekking map (down jacket and sleeping bag are to be returned after trip completion)
  8. All meals [Breakfast, Lunch and Dinner / main course] during the trek as per the itinerary
  9. Down jacket, four seasonal sleeping bag, duffel bag and trekking map (down jacket and sleeping bag are to be returned after trip completion)
  10. Comprehensive first aid medical kit carried by the guide
  11. Necessary paper works, all government and local taxes
I purposefully selected tours without porters because I wanted to carry my own luggage by myself. But all the tours suggested that I can have porter services as add-on to the trip.


I believe these are the minimum required items for hikers like us to trek to EBC. So I am sure I would be happy choosing any of these agencies. But they have some different characteristics here and there. To optimize the choice of agencies, I looked at some of their statistics. Insurance

Travel Protection Explorer Plan from World Nomads was the one of the few travel insurances that cover high altitude evacuation. We wanted to have some insurance just in case if we need to evacuate with helicopter in high altitude (which is not cheep without insurance). We only purchased this travel insurance for 13 days DAY2 (25 Apr 2018) to DAY 14 (7 May 2018) when we are in altitude higher than 3,000m.
Note: We have another regular travel insurance, which does not cover high-altitude evacuation, as we are foreigners working in US.
The documentation of Travel Insurance: Activities, sports & adventures covered by World Nomads says:
Mountaineering, high altitude climbing or expeditions (up to 22,965 feet/7,000 meters)
As we are only planning to go up to 5,200 meters, this insurance must be suffice!
